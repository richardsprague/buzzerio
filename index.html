Approach

I can help you with emotional problems by finding how your thoughts, feelings, and actions are connected. My years of experience have shown how people can change their life by changing how they think. My goal is to help you be the best you can be in any situation. I use things like talking with you and helping you talk to yourself in a positive way.

I also use things like relaxation exercises, and teaching you to be more aware of your thoughts and feelings through self-instruction (such as distraction techniques and positive self-talk), relaxation and biofeedback, identification and refutation of negative thoughts, exercises at home, exposure therapy and mindfulness.

My motivations

  • Modify your deepest counterproductive beliefs, favoring a lasting change over time.
  • Help you achieve your goals.
  • Teach you to think strategically.

I am results-oriented, so I am capable of supporting you in defining your goals and overcoming the challenges that may be holding you back from where you want to arrive.

On Mobility

I often deal with mobility, i.e., movement in general and in particular the expatriation of individuals or entire families. All this builds on my personal experience: for many years, I moved from one country and continent to another. I have directly experienced what it means to adapt to new cultures, live with loneliness for sometimes long periods, not have the opportunity to cultivate stable and lasting relationships, and, above all, undergo a personal readjustment.

This involves becoming aware of the place where one is and, consequently, overcoming the feeling of being alone and uncomfortable in an unfamiliar environment.

Children, for example, despite having great adaptability, need to understand the meaning of it, assimilate it, and then develop it: this will give them an extra “gear” and help them in life to face the pitfalls that every change entails, more quickly and with more serenity.

Adults, on the other hand, must develop and refine their adaptability and mental flexibility more and more following the fracture of a balance achieved elsewhere, and called into question by every change. This process is not easy for some, especially since in a family, for example, one’s own balance is linked to that of the other family members. A psychotherapist’s assistance is essential to facilitate the future and transform what arises as discomfort into a point of strength.
